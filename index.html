I study how machines learn to recognize — and standardize — a face, a body, a way of relating. About

I'm a researcher working at the intersection of critical digital humanities and AI governance. This summer I'm an AI Workstreams Research Intern at Harvard's Berkman Klein Center for Internet & Society, contributing to the AI and Human Experience initiative — researching how young people form relationships with AI companion systems, what those relationships mean for socio-emotional development and adult relationship formation, and how design and policy should respond.

I just finished an MPhil in Digital Humanities at the University of Cambridge, where my dissertation examined how far-right movements use online beauty aesthetics and looksmaxxing culture as a vector for ideological recruitment and platform evasion. Before that, I studied English Language, Media & Communications at the University of Rochester, graduating with Honors and a Political Science minor.

My method draws on feminist media theory (Haraway, Federici, Wolf, Kristeva, Creed) and platform/visual culture critique (Steyerl, Sekula, Crawford & Paglen, Zylinska) to read AI systems not as neutral infrastructure, but as aesthetic and ideological actors. I'm currently weighing a JD and a PhD, with an eye toward staying in the research-policy space long term — somewhere that lets me keep writing, researching, and arguing about how technology governs bodies and culture.

Research

Selected writing, ordered roughly by how far each project pushed the next — from an undergraduate thesis on horror and the monstrous feminine, through a dissertation on far-right beauty aesthetics, toward current work on how young people form relationships with AI companions.

In progress · BKC / AIHE research

The Future of Youth Relationships and AI Companions

Berkman Klein Center, 2026

An examination of how young people form relationships with AI companion systems, and what those relationships mean for socio-emotional development and the formation of adult relationships. Frames the central question as rehearsal versus replacement — whether companions scaffold human connection or substitute for it — and asks how design and policy should respond.

note Developed within the AI and Human Experience initiative, toward a framing article and a youth relationship taxonomy.
Dissertation · MPhil Digital Humanities

The Faces of Fascism: Online Beauty Aesthetics as a Tool for Far-Right Ideology

University of Cambridge, 2026

Examines how far-right movements deploy online beauty aesthetics, looksmaxxing culture, and visual identity to recruit, normalize ideology, and evade platform moderation — reading algorithmic beauty standards as a vector for far-right values through feminist media theory and critical digital humanities methods.

method Feminist media theory + critical digital humanities, close reading platform logic alongside political aesthetics.
Essay · First Class (74), in preparation for journal submission

Toward the Perfect Face: Platform Logic, Looksmaxxing, and Networked Identity

University of Cambridge, 2025

Argues that looksmaxxing presents itself as radical individual agency while enacting algorithmic conformity and identity dissolution, tracing the "Instagram face" through Steyerl, Sekula, Crawford & Paglen, and Rothwell.

note The seed of the dissertation — first attempt at theorizing algorithmic beauty as ideology rather than aesthetics alone.
Honors senior thesis · English, advised by Sarah Higley

Becoming the Monster: Beauty, Control, and the Monstrous Feminine in Contemporary Horror

University of Rochester, 2025

Examines how contemporary horror films critique the beauty industry's demands on women's bodies through the monstrous feminine, drawing on Creed, Kristeva, and Wolf in analysis of Black Swan, Helter Skelter, and The Substance.

note Where the throughline starts: beauty standards as a disciplinary, gendered technology of control.
